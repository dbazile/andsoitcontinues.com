Hi, I'm Dave I build clean interfaces for the web

Who I am

  • Father and Husband
  • Veteran of the United States Marine Corps
  • 60% Software Developer, 40% UI/UX Acolyte

What I do

By day, I build software for my employer and its clients. By night, I read up on anything relating to user interface development and research any software engineering problems encountered during the day. I am also working towards a masters degree in User Experience Design. Occasionally, I play console videogames.

Once Upon a Time...

As a teenager, I was deeply interested in web design. With the exception of videogames, there wasn't much else I spent time on. In fact, one interest served to reinforce the other in many ways.

My earliest programming undertaking was born out of the desire to correct several UI inefficiencies of a Miva-scripted bulletin board application on a videogame fan club website. Achieving that goal and receiving many accolades from my fellow gamers for solving that long-lived problem, that curiosity shifted to full-blown web design and development.

Armed with hubris and momentum from my first coding victory, I went forth overhauling legions of GeoCities-quality HTML 3.0 websites to feature custom-made Photoshop graphics, CSS and coherent color schemes. Since the clientele was invariably my fellow broke, unemployed gaming teenage peers, I never charged anything for designs and when I hosted others' fan pages, I did so free of charge. I did this (along with actually going to school every now and then) right up until I joined the service.

Sites I frequent

I'm nowhere near as active online as I was in my teenage years, but if you're so inclined, you can find stuff written or created by me on GitHub, Twitter and Instagram under my handle spoonchucks. It should be said that when trying to come up with an internet pseudonym, one will encounter much difficulty finding one that fits all of the following adjectives: unique, anonymous, clever, timeless, unoffensive.

I also lurk on Hacker News.

What is the purpose of this site?

Much of the career advice I've read from people I respect in the software development field trends toward the sentiment that all developers should have a blog. The one that most sticks out in my mind is this blog post by Scott Hanselman.

I'll admit that as the years have passed, I've gotten hyper-vigilant about what I put up on the web—rivaled possibly only by folks ducking the law or the mafia—and I wear this as a badge of honor in a time that public over-sharing seems to be the norm. But, I do need a place to host technical experimentation, lessons learned, sample data and my portfolio. I'll try to keep it professional!
